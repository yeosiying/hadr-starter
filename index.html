Know about a disaster that will hurt people — without watching three websites all day.

A single always-on service watches the world's disaster feeds, folds them into one event per real-world crisis, and publishes what matters to a page you open on demand. It alerts on modeled human impact — not raw magnitude — and when it's quiet, you can trust the quiet.

What is this

A personal early-warning page for humanitarian disasters.

Three authoritative feeds — GDACS, USGS, and ReliefWeb — each report disasters in their own format, on their own schedule, with their own idea of what counts. None of them push; all of them have to be checked. HADR Monitor does the checking. It polls each feed on its own cadence, archives every payload before it's read, and merges the overlap so one real-world earthquake shows up once — not three times under three names — with each source's claim visible underneath.

The result is a single local web page: current alerts at the top, recent changes below, and a health banner that tells you when a feed has gone dark. You open it when you want to know. There's nothing to log into and nothing to configure.

Why it works this way — and where it's headed

Because magnitude is not the same thing as harm.

An M7.5 in the empty ocean is a non-event. An M6.2 under a crowded city is a catastrophe. A raw seismic feed can't tell you which one just happened — it only reports physics. So HADR Monitor doesn't trigger on physics. It triggers on modeled impact: GDACS Orange/Red, USGS PAGER at yellow or above, plus a fast provisional path for a large quake near people while the impact models are still catching up.

The signal worth interrupting your day for is “this will affect people,” not “the ground moved.” That single conviction shapes every other decision — the trigger rules, the hazard scope, even what gets stored silently.

The second conviction is that silence has to be trustworthy. A monitoring tool that fails quietly is worse than no tool — you'd rely on it and be blindsided. So a stalled feed never disappears into calm: it turns the page's banner red. No alerts and no banner means genuinely nothing is happening.

Direction: the foundations are built to grow. ReliefWeb enrichment lights up the moment its access approval lands (adding human-written confirmation and GLIDE cross-references). Every payload is archived, so any past alert can be replayed and any future parsing bug reproduced. The thresholds and windows are configuration, not code — meant to be tuned against real traffic once the service has been running a while. It's deliberately a foundation, not a finished dashboard.

How the pieces fit

Three feeds, read as layers — not as rivals.

They aren't redundant copies of the same news. Each one answers a different question, and later layers confirm or correct the earlier ones.

minutes USGS — did the ground move? Fast seismic detection. Physical severity only, and its records change — quakes get revised, re-keyed, even deleted.
~25 min GDACS — will it hurt people? Modeled exposure × vulnerability across all hazard types. Green / Orange / Red is the number that decides whether you're alerted.
hours–days ReliefWeb — what do humans say? Editorial confirmation and the GLIDE join key. Enriches an event that already exists — it never raises an alert on its own.

Who it's for

One person who needs to notice, quickly, when the world needs help.

The reader

A single HADR-interested person — the owner. No team, no accounts, no multi-tenancy. They visit a page on their own machine; a morning snapshot is written for them at 08:30. It's tuned to personal-tool reliability, with the one hard rule that silence must be honest.

The decision it speeds up

“Is anything happening right now that I should pay attention to?” Instead of opening three sites, cross-referencing whether they're describing the same event, and judging severity by eye, you glance at one page that has already filtered for human impact and merged the duplicates. Seconds, not a morning ritual.

What it deliberately does not do

The scope it refuses — on purpose.

Every one of these is a decision, not a gap. Staying small is how the part that matters stays trustworthy.

  • It doesn't push. No Telegram, email, or Slack. It's a page you pull, served on localhost — nothing public, nothing to secure.
  • It isn't multi-user. One owner, one machine. No auth, no teams, no ops-center dashboards.
  • It doesn't rank the world by severity. ReliefWeb attention isn't importance, and physical magnitude is never treated as impact.
  • It doesn't forecast or analyze. It reports what the feeds assert — no predictions, no modeling of its own.
  • It stays quiet on some hazards. Volcanoes and droughts are stored but never alerted; wildfires only at Red (they're most of the feed's volume).
  • It won't let ReliefWeb trigger an alert. That feed confirms and enriches — it never fires.
  • It doesn't prune. Everything is kept indefinitely; the archive is the audit trail and the replay-test corpus.
